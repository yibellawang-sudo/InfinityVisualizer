What is a Fractal?

A fractal is a shape ot pattern that repeats its structure at different scales, a propoerty call self-similarity. Zoom in on a fractal, and you'll keep discovering new details that resemble the whole. This makes fractals both mathematically fascinating and visually stunning

Fractals are infinitely complex patterns created by repeating a simple process over and over in a continuous feedback loop. Driven by recursion, they are essentially the images of dynamic systems, or, the picture of Chaos. Geometrically, fractals exist in between our familiar dimensions, forming shapes that are neither one nor two dimensional, but something in between.

Fractals are everywhere in nature. Examples include:

  • Trees and plants
  • Rivers and coastlines
  • Mountains and clouds
  • Seashells and hurricanes

    • Abstract fractals, such as the Mandelbrot Set, are generated by computer programs that repeat a simple mathemtical equation endlessly, revealing intricate patterns at every level of magnification.

    Key Characteristics of Fractals

    • Self-Similarity: The pattern repeates itself at smaller and smaller scales
    • Infinite Detail: Fractals possess intricate structure at all levels of magnification
    • Recursive Structure: A feedback loop creates the repeating pattern.
    • Fractal Dimension: Fractals often have a non-integer dimension, reflecting their complexity.
