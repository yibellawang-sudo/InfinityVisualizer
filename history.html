First, the early origins

The concept of fractals dates back to the 19th century, when mathematicians like Georg Cantor and Helge von Koch explored strange “pathological” objects. Cantor’s set, created by repeatedly removing the middle third of a line, and Koch’s snowflake curve revealed shapes with infinite perimeter but finite area — breaking traditional geometry.

Next, the pioneers

These early “monsters” fascinated but also unsettled mathematicians. Later, Gaston Julia and Pierre Fatou studied the iteration of complex functions in the early 20th century, laying the groundwork for the Julia sets — intricate structures that hinted at the hidden complexity of dynamical systems.

Then, Benoît Mandelbrot revolutionizes the field

In the 1970s, Benoît Mandelbrot unified these ideas under the term fractals, meaning “broken” or “fragmented.” Using computer graphics, he visualized the Mandelbrot set, showing how simple formulas could generate infinite complexity. This gave mathematics a new language to describe roughness, irregularity, and self-similarity in nature.

Fractals and Chaos Theory

Fractals are deeply tied to chaos theory, which studies systems highly sensitive to initial conditions — the so-called “butterfly effect.” Strange attractors like the Lorenz attractor reveal fractal geometry hidden within weather systems, population models, and fluid dynamics. Fractals became the geometry of chaos.

Today, the modern impact

Fractals now influence computer graphics, art, medicine, finance, and physics. From modeling coastlines and blood vessels to generating realistic landscapes in movies and video games, fractals bridge mathematics and the natural world — showing that beauty and order can emerge from chaos.
