Early Thinking

In the 19th century, mathematicians such as Georg Cantor and Helge von Koch explored "pathological" sets and curves. Cantor's set, created by repeatedly removing the middle third of a line, and Koch's snowflake, with its infinite perimeter yet finite area, challenged classical geometry and hinted at the infinite complexity of simple rules.

Pioneers

Gaston Julia and Pierre Fatou in the early 20th century studied iterations of complex functions, producing intricate patterns that foreshadowed modern fractals. Their work laid the foundation for understanding how repeated operations can generate infinitely detailed structures.

Mandelbrot

Benoît Mandelbrot coined the term "fractal" in the 1970s and visualized the Mandelbrot set using computers. His work revealed how simple formulas could create infinitely complex and self-similar shapes, revolutionizing the way mathematicians and scientists describe natural patterns.

Chaos Theory

Fractals are closely tied to chaos theory, which studies systems highly sensitive to initial conditions. Strange attractors like the Lorenz attractor reveal fractal structures hidden in weather, fluid dynamics, and population models. This demonstrates how order and patterns emerge from seemingly chaotic systems.

Modern Advances

Today, fractals influence fields ranging from computer graphics and art to medicine and finance. They model natural shapes like coastlines and blood vessels, generate realistic landscapes in movies, and provide new insights into complex systems. Fractals continue to bridge mathematics with the natural and digital world.
